# "FCAJ Sharing Session" Event Summary

### Event Objectives

The FCAJ Sharing Session was organized to help participants broaden their perspectives on learning, working, and applying AI in practice. Through sharing from experienced seniors, the event not only focused on technical knowledge but also emphasized personal development thinking, effective learning methods, professional work habits, and how to use AI to boost productivity.

The event's goal was to help students and participants understand that learning technology isn't just about absorbing new knowledge—it also requires maintaining motivation, knowing how to ask questions, using AI tools properly, and building the right mindset for real work environments.

Additionally, the event gave participants a chance to reflect on their own learning and work habits. In an era of rapid AI development, learners can't just passively absorb knowledge; they need to actively explore, experiment, ask questions, and turn tools into assistants for their learning and work.

Through four main sharing sessions, participants accessed highly practical topics like making learning an engaging habit, effective prompt engineering with AI, the right mindset for work, and the BMAD Method for AI-assisted software development.

### Key Highlights

#### How to Get Addicted to Learning Like You Get Addicted to Social Media

Anh Long's sharing focused on a very relatable issue for students today: why we can spend hours on social media but easily lose focus when studying. The content emphasized that learning often feels hard to start because results don't come immediately, while social media provides quick stimulation, constant feedback, and instant entertainment.

An important takeaway was that to sustain long-term learning, we shouldn't rely solely on willpower. Instead, learners should redesign their environment and methods to make learning easier to start. For example, break big goals into small tasks, set short but consistent study times, track daily progress, and create a sense of "completion" after each small step.

Anh Long also emphasized that learning can become more engaging by applying elements similar to social media, like streaks, progress points, checklists, personal challenges, and learning communities. When learners see their daily progress, learning gradually shifts from feeling forced to feeling voluntary.

A very practical point was that learners need to reduce friction when starting to study. If every study session requires spending lots of time preparing materials, opening the computer, finding lessons, or choosing content, the brain will easily procrastinate. Conversely, if materials are prepped, daily learning goals are clear, and study time is broken down, starting becomes much easier.

Additionally, learning also needs quick feedback. When using social media, users constantly get likes, comments, new videos, or new information. For learning, learners can create their own feedback by taking notes on what they've learned, marking completed lessons, testing themselves with short questions, or sharing learned content with others.

The biggest lesson from this section was that to study well, you don't necessarily have to study a lot right away—what matters is building a system that gets you back to learning every day. When learning becomes a habit, learners no longer rely too much on temporary inspiration.

#### How to Handle Prompts and Work Effectively with AI

Anh Thịnh's sharing helped participants better understand how to use AI through prompts. In practice, many people often enter very short or generic requests and expect AI to return accurate results. However, the quality of AI's answers depends heavily on how the user provides input information.

An effective prompt needs all the elements: goal, context, related data, output requirements, desired format, and constraints to follow. Without context, AI easily misinterprets the problem or creates answers that don't align with real needs. So instead of just asking a simple question, users should clearly describe what they're doing, what results they want, and what those results will be used for.

This content also helped participants understand that working with AI isn't a one-ask-and-done process. Prompt handling often requires multiple iterations: set initial request, check results, find what's wrong, add information, and ask AI to revise. This is an important skill because AI is increasingly used in learning, report writing, coding, data analysis, and product building.

A notable point was that prompts aren't just for asking AI for answers—they can also be used to assign roles to AI. For example, when writing a report, you can ask AI to act as an academic instructor; when fixing code, ask AI to act as a senior developer; when analyzing a system, ask AI to act as a solution architect. Defining clear roles helps answers have a more appropriate perspective.

Additionally, Anh Thịnh emphasized that users need to control AI's output. They shouldn't accept all results blindly, but should check accuracy, suitability to requirements, and logical consistency. Especially in coding or academic reports, AI can help a lot but still requires human review, revision, and responsibility for the final result.

From this sharing, participants realized that AI doesn't completely replace human thinking. Users still need to evaluate results, check reasonableness, and adjust requests. Someone who can write good prompts will use AI more effectively, save more time, and create more practically valuable results.

#### Mindset for Work

Anh Khang's sharing focused on the mindset needed when entering real work environments. Unlike learning environments, where students often get clear assignments and specific guidance, work environments require everyone to be more proactive, more responsible, and know how to solve problems on their own.

An important mindset emphasized was ownership spirit. When receiving a task, workers shouldn't just complete the assigned job—they need to understand the final goal of that task, who it affects, and what level of results is needed. Ownership thinking helps each individual work more responsibly and not blame others when facing difficulties.

Additionally, Anh Khang mentioned the need to learn from feedback. At work, receiving feedback is normal and necessary for improvement. Instead of seeing feedback as personal criticism, workers should see it as data to improve skills, communication, and work quality.

Another practical lesson was the need to communicate clearly when working in teams. If facing difficulties, don't stay silent for too long—proactively report the situation, state the problem, and suggest a solution direction. This helps the team track progress, avoid missed deadlines, and improve collaboration efficiency.

This section also helped participants understand that at work, answers aren't always available. Some problems require self-research, self-experimentation, re-asking requirements, and self-proposing solutions. Someone with a good mindset won't just say "I don't know how to do this"—they'll clearly explain what they've tried, where they're stuck, and what support they need.

Beyond technical skills, work attitude is also a very important factor. Workers need to be on time, keep promises, update progress, note down requirements, and know how to prioritize important work. These things are simple but show professionalism and build trust with colleagues, managers, or customers.

Through this sharing, participants understood that technical competence is important, but work attitude, responsibility spirit, learning ability, and communication are also factors that determine long-term career development.

#### BMAD Method

Chị Thảo's sharing introduced the BMAD Method, a method to make AI-assisted product development more structured. Instead of just using AI for random Q&A or asking AI to write code immediately, BMAD aims to break the software development process into clearer steps like defining requirements, analyzing business, designing architecture, breaking down work, implementing, and testing.

The highlight of BMAD Method is its process-based approach. Before starting to code, users need to clarify the problem, product goals, target users, main features, and technical constraints. After that, AI can be used as a support team with different roles, like Business Analyst, Product Manager, Architect, Developer, or QA.

This approach helps reduce "gut-feel" product development. If you just ask AI to "make an app" or "code a feature," the result might work but easily lacks logic, is hard to scale, and hard to maintain. Conversely, with clear requirement documents, architecture, user stories, and testing criteria, the development process is easier to control.

An important part of BMAD Method is separating the development process stages. In the early stage, workers need to focus on understanding the problem and defining requirements. Then move to designing solutions, choosing technologies, breaking tasks, and implementing. This approach helps avoid coding too early only to have to revise a lot later because you didn't understand the requirements correctly.

BMAD also helps learners understand that AI can help better when given specific tasks. Instead of asking AI to make the entire product at once, you can break it into steps like analyzing requirements, writing user stories, proposing database schemas, designing APIs, creating interfaces, writing test cases, and reviewing code. When tasks are clear, AI's results are easier to check and use.

This sharing also helped participants understand that AI is only truly powerful when placed in a reasonable work process. AI can help speed things up, but humans still need to play the roles of guiding, checking, and making decisions. So the BMAD Method isn't just a way to use AI—it's also a systematic product-building mindset.

### What You Learned

#### Building Sustainable Learning Habits

Through Anh Long's sharing, participants learned that effective learning doesn't just depend on temporary motivation. To sustain long-term learning, you need to build a learning system that suits you. Breaking down goals, creating checklists, tracking progress, and maintaining daily habits will make learning easier.

Participants also realized that learning shouldn't be seen as a heavy task. If you know how to create a sense of progress, challenges, and small rewards, learning can become as engaging as how social media keeps users hooked.

Especially, learners need to know how to start with small, realistic goals. Instead of forcing yourself to study for hours straight, you can start with 25 minutes of focused study, then take a short break and continue. When consistent, accumulated knowledge will increase over time.

#### Knowing How to Use AI Smarter

From Anh Thịnh's sharing, participants understood that prompts are a very important factor when working with AI. A good prompt isn't just a question—it's a request with full context, goals, data, and output criteria.

The takeaway was that you shouldn't use AI passively. Users need to know how to check, critique, and adjust results. AI can save time, but final quality still depends on human guidance and evaluation ability.

Additionally, participants learned that AI is suitable for supporting thinking, suggesting solution directions, and speeding up work, but you shouldn't rely completely on AI. When using AI to learn, you need to understand the essence of the problem instead of just copying answers.

#### Preparing a Work Mindset

Through Anh Khang's sharing, participants learned that when going to work, attitude and mindset are no less important than technical skills. Someone with a good mindset needs to know how to proactively take tasks, be responsible for results, communicate clearly, and not be afraid to receive feedback.

This is a very necessary lesson for students preparing for internships or work. Enterprise environments require adaptability, learning spirit, and professionalism in handling work.

Participants also understood that at work, you don't just need to do your part correctly—you also need to understand how your work contributes to the overall goal. When understanding the overall goal, each individual will know how to prioritize better and make more appropriate decisions.

#### Developing Process-Based Products with BMAD

From Chị Thảo's sharing, participants understood that when using AI to build software, you shouldn't start by writing code right away. Before that, you need steps to analyze requirements, define features, design architecture, and break down work.

The BMAD Method helps learners see AI as a support tool throughout the entire product development process, not just a code-generation tool. This is especially useful for big projects, theses, or real products that need stability and scalability.

The important lesson was that a good product needs a clear process. If you have specific requirements, design, tasks, and testing criteria, the work process will be less chaotic, easier to collaborate on, and the final result will also be of higher quality.

### Applying to Work and Learning

The knowledge from the sharing session can be directly applied to the learning process, personal projects, and preparation for real work.

For learning, you can apply Anh Long's method by breaking down learning goals day by day. For example, instead of setting a goal to "learn AWS" or "learn coding," you can break it into small tasks like completing a lab, reading part of a document, noting down key concepts, or practicing a specific feature. This makes learning clearer and easier to maintain.

For using AI, you can apply Anh Thịnh's knowledge when writing prompts for ChatGPT or other AI tools. When you need AI to help write reports, fix code, analyze errors, or design systems, you need to provide full context like the technology being used, goals to achieve, errors encountered, desired format, and constraints to follow.

For example, when doing an AWS report, instead of just asking "help me write about IAM," you can write more clearly: "I'm doing an internship report on AWS IAM, target readers are 3rd-year students, please write the concept section, main features, practical examples, and key takeaways, in an academic but easy-to-understand tone." Writing prompts this way will make results more aligned with needs.

For work environments, lessons from Anh Khang can be applied during internships or team work. When receiving a task, you need to ask for clear requirements, confirm deadlines, update progress, and proactively report problems if facing difficulties. This makes work more transparent and builds trust with the team.

For software projects, the BMAD Method can be applied to build a clearer process. Before coding, the team can write requirement descriptions, define user stories, design overall architecture, break tasks, and set testing criteria. Then use AI to support each part like writing code, creating documents, checking logic, or suggesting improvements.

These lessons can also be applied to personal projects or graduation theses. When building a website, application, or cloud system, students should start by defining the problem, target users, main features, data to manage, and workflow. Then choose appropriate technologies to implement.
