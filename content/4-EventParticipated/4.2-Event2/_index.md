### Key Highlights

#### Context Is Everything: Making AI Actually Work for You

The first session emphasized that context is the core factor determining the quality of AI responses. In practice, many users feel AI is underperforming because the model is not smart enough — but the real cause is usually that users provide overly vague requests, lacking clear goals, background data, and evaluation criteria.

The speaker clarified that context is not just a few extra lines of description — it is the full picture that helps AI correctly understand the problem at hand. A good request should clearly state what the user wants to achieve, which data matters, what format the output should follow, and what constraints must be respected. With sufficient context, AI can produce more accurate, relevant, and actionable responses.

The session also introduced the concept of a **Second AI Brain** — representing the evolution of AI from a one-off Q&A tool into a system capable of storing, organizing, retrieving, and using information over the long term. This is a useful approach for students and developers building systems to support learning, note-taking, document analysis, and personal knowledge management.

#### Friendly AI Assistant with Amazon Quick

This session introduced Amazon Quick as a platform for building friendly AI assistants capable of helping users analyze data, generate insights, and automate workflows. The key message was that AI is not just a chat tool — it can become a genuine assistant in the workplace.

Key components introduced included **Quick Chat Agent**, which helps users explore data and analyze information; **Quick Flows**, which allows building intelligent workflows using natural language without any coding; **Quick Spaces**, which turns individual knowledge into a collaborative team environment; and **Quick Sight**, which supports building dashboards and reports from raw data using natural language.

Through this session, attendees recognized that AI assistants can reduce repetitive work, speed up decision-making, and improve collaboration efficiency in enterprise settings. This is a highly practical application direction for organizations looking to leverage data and automate internal processes.

#### From Edge To Origin: CloudFront as Your Foundation

This session gave attendees a deeper understanding of Amazon CloudFront's role in modern system architecture. CloudFront is not just a CDN for content delivery — it can serve as a critical foundation layer that optimizes performance, cost, security, and reliability for applications.

The presentation showed that CloudFront can be applied to many types of workloads, from static websites and web applications to APIs and high-traffic systems. Using CloudFront reduces load on origin servers, shortens response times, optimizes data transfer costs, and improves the end-user experience.

Beyond performance, CloudFront also supports a range of security capabilities including HTTPS, access control, and integration with protective services like AWS WAF and Shield. Mechanisms such as caching, origin failover, custom error pages, and a global edge infrastructure also help systems maintain better stability during incidents or sudden traffic spikes.

#### 36 hrs with LotusHacks – Building UTMorpho from Idea to Reality

Team VIB's session offered a real-world perspective on building a product in a hackathon environment. The team recounted their journey through LotusHacks and developing UTMorpho in 36 hours — from having no clear idea to delivering a demoable product.

A notable point was that the team did not start with a perfect idea. Instead, they began by observing real problems, brainstorming, identifying pain points, and gradually shaping the product. This demonstrated that a good product usually starts with truly understanding the user's problem, and only then selecting the right technology and building the appropriate solution.

During development, the team faced time pressure, technical challenges, task division, feature integration, and demo preparation. Through this, the session helped attendees understand that a hackathon is not just a coding competition — it is an environment for developing product thinking, teamwork, time management, adaptability, and the ability to present ideas effectively.

#### Non-Determinism of "Deterministic" LLM Settings

This session helped attendees better understand how large language models generate output. LLMs do not produce a complete answer all at once — they generate content token by token, based on the probability distribution of the next token.

A key point emphasized was that the assumption **temperature = 0** will always produce identical results does not always hold in practice. While temperature = 0 reduces randomness in the token selection process, outputs can still vary due to optimizations during inference, parallel processing, batching, or differences in computation across hardware infrastructure.

This has significant practical implications when deploying LLMs in real products. For systems requiring high reliability, developers should not rely solely on prompts — they need to build additional mechanisms for testing, validation, structured output, fallback logic, and monitoring to reduce the risk of inconsistent AI outputs.

#### Enterprise-Grade Multi-Agent System: The Case of Startup Credit Scoring

The final session presented an enterprise-grade multi-agent system through a startup credit scoring case study. The talk highlighted the major difference between traditional financial data and startup data. While banking systems typically rely on credit history, collateral, and stable revenue, startups have richer multi-dimensional data such as founding team, market opportunity, product, traction, and growth potential.

Because this problem involves many different evaluation perspectives, a single agent may not be capable of comprehensive analysis. A multi-agent model was proposed as a virtual credit committee, where each agent takes on a specialized role:

- **Financial Analyst**
- **Market Analyst**
- **Team Evaluator**
- **Risk Assessor**
- **Compliance Agent**
- **Manager Agent**

This approach enables better specialization, cross-checking of results, audit trail creation, and ensures critical enterprise requirements such as guardrails, compliance, security, and operational efficiency. It is a clear example showing that AI in enterprise settings must not only be intelligent — it must also be safe, controllable, and scalable.

### Key Takeaways

#### AI Usage Mindset

Through the event, attendees learned that using AI effectively depends not just on the model, but heavily on how the problem is framed. A good prompt must be built on a foundation of clear context, including the objective, relevant data, output requirements, constraints, and evaluation criteria.

Attendees also recognized that AI should not be used passively. Rather than simply asking and accepting results, users need to know how to verify, refine, provide additional information, and assess the relevance of responses. This is an important skill as AI becomes increasingly integrated into learning and work.

#### Knowledge of AI Assistants and Automation

From the Amazon Quick session, attendees understood that AI assistants can support many practical tasks such as data analysis, dashboard creation, workflow building, and team collaboration. This opens up an important direction for applying AI in enterprise settings — reducing time spent on manual tasks, accelerating decision-making, and improving operational efficiency.

In particular, the ability to create workflows using natural language shows that AI is enabling non-technical users to participate in automation processes. This is a significant trend in digital transformation, where technology serves not only developers but also business users.

#### Cloud Architecture and System Optimization

Through the CloudFront content, attendees learned that a good system must not only run — it must be fast, secure, stable, and cost-efficient. CloudFront improves the user experience by bringing content closer to users while reducing pressure on origin servers.

Attendees also gained a deeper appreciation for the importance of caching, security capabilities, origin failover, and global edge infrastructure in production systems. These are essential concepts for anyone studying backend development, DevOps, or cloud architecture.

#### Product Thinking and Teamwork

The story of building UTMorpho in 36 hours helped attendees understand that product development is not just about writing code — it is a process of defining the problem, choosing a solution direction, dividing tasks, testing, demoing, and communicating the product's value.

A key lesson was that in a time-constrained environment, a team must know how to prioritize core features rather than trying to do too much. Clear communication, continuous coordination, and maintaining a shared goal are the deciding factors in delivering a product on time.

#### Deeper Understanding of LLMs

The non-determinism session gave attendees a more realistic view of LLMs. While LLMs are highly capable of generating content, they are fundamentally probabilistic systems and can produce different results under certain conditions.

Therefore, when integrating LLMs into real applications — especially systems involving critical data or business decisions — output control mechanisms are essential. Structured output, validation, repeated testing, and fallback logic are necessary elements for building more reliable systems.

#### Enterprise-Grade Mindset for AI Deployment

Through the multi-agent credit scoring case study, attendees learned that an enterprise-grade AI system must not only function well — it must also ensure security, compliance, auditability, scalability, and return on investment.

Multi-agent systems are a suitable approach for complex problems, as they allow tasks to be broken down by specialization, reduce the risk of depending on a single agent, and produce results with higher explainability. This is an important mindset when developing AI solutions in finance, banking, or large enterprise environments.

### Applying to Work

The knowledge from FCAJ Community Day can be directly applied to learning, personal projects, and real-world work.

For software learning and development, attendees can apply **context engineering** thinking when working with AI. When asking AI to help write code, fix bugs, design systems, or create reports, it is important to provide complete information such as the technology in use, the goal to achieve, the error encountered, the required output format, and any constraints to follow. This leads to more accurate AI results and less time spent on revisions.

For website, backend, or cloud projects, knowledge of **Amazon CloudFront** can be applied to speed up page loading, reduce server load, protect the origin, and optimize operational costs. When deploying real systems, CloudFront can be combined with caching strategies, HTTPS, WAF, Shield, signed URLs, and origin failover to improve both performance and security.

For data analysis and automation tasks, the **Amazon Quick** model can serve as a reference for building assistants that support report aggregation, dashboard creation, insight analysis, or team collaboration. This is a practical application direction in enterprise settings, especially for repetitive tasks or those requiring data processing from multiple sources.

When working in teams or participating in hackathons, the Team VIB process can be applied: start from a real problem, brainstorm with a clear goal, select core features, divide tasks clearly, build an MVP quickly, test continuously, and prepare a compelling demo. This approach keeps the team focused on product value rather than being scattered by too many ideas.

For complex AI systems, knowledge of LLMs and **multi-agent systems** helps attendees understand how to design safer systems. Rather than relying on a single model or prompt, it is important to add validation, guardrails, audit trails, monitoring, and output control mechanisms. This is essential when AI is used in domains with high requirements for reliability and compliance.
